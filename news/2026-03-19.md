# NVIDIA Engineers Jump In to Secure OpenClaw

OpenClaw Gets Enterprise-Grade Security from NVIDIA Engineers

The open-source AI agent platform just got a major vote of confidence — and some serious security muscle.

### 🔥 The Big Stories

**NVIDIA Engineers Jump In to Secure OpenClaw**

The GPU giant is lending engineering talent to help triage security advisories for the open-source AI agent platform that's been taking over developer Twitter. This isn't just PR — it's a signal that NVIDIA sees OpenClaw as critical infrastructure for the AI agent future they're betting on. When a hardware company starts contributing to an agent platform's security, that's enterprise adoption validation in real time. The timing coincides with OpenClaw's new Chrome DevTools integration that lets agents control live browser sessions with full access to tabs, cookies, and logins. [Worth watching: how quickly other enterprise vendors follow NVIDIA's lead here](https://x.com/openclaw/status/2032694713493406060).

**The 2028 Deadline Leak**

Paul Graham caught an interesting slip from an OpenAI employee: "Anything made before 2028 is going to be valuable." While PG downplays the specific year, the implication is clear — OpenAI internally expects another major capability jump that makes pre-2028 human work significantly more valuable as collectibles. This tracks with the pattern we've seen: each model generation doesn't just improve incrementally, it obsoletes entire categories of human output. The comment came up during a conversation about buying rare old things as a hedge against AI automation. [Smart founders are already thinking about which parts of their business become more valuable, not less, as AI capabilities explode](https://x.com/paulg/status/2034551423149474224).

**AI "Psychosis" Becomes a Recognized Pattern**

Ethan Mollick is documenting something many of us have seen firsthand: the inevitable "AI psychosis" that hits professionals when they finally grok what's possible. The pattern is intense, sleepless projects using AI to tackle impossibly complex work in their domain, often productive but temporary. This matters because it's becoming the standard way experts discover AI's true potential in their field. The sleepless coding marathons with Claude, the weekend-long research binges with GPT-5.4 — this isn't just hype, it's how domain expertise gets turbocharged by AI capability.

### 💡 Builder's Corner

**UNSLOP: The Anti-Generic AI Tool** — Matt Shumer open-sourced a tool that trains AI models to recognize and avoid generating "slop" — the generic, obvious patterns that make AI output feel robotic. Feed it examples of bad landing pages, and it builds skills to never generate similar patterns again. This addresses one of the biggest practical problems in AI-generated content: everything starting to sound the same.
