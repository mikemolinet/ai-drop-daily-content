# The Great Inversion: Software Is Being Rebuilt for Machines

For as long as software has existed, it's been built by humans, for humans. Every interface, every dashboard, every API doc — designed so a person can understand what's happening and decide what to do next.

That constraint is breaking. On both sides. Simultaneously.

AI agents are building the software now. And increasingly, AI agents are the ones using it too. When you remove humans from both ends of the equation, everything we assumed about how technology should work starts to unravel.

This week, this idea hit escape velocity. Eric Schmidt co-authored a piece arguing we're entering the era of "machines building for machines." Jensen Huang told GTC that every SaaS company will become a "GaaS" company. a16z published a defense of software companies while acknowledging $2 trillion in SaaS market cap just evaporated. And buried in the data, a stat that makes all of it feel real: 80% of databases on Neon are now created by AI agents, not humans.

Something fundamental is shifting. Let's break it down.

### 🔄 The Production Side: Built BY Agents

The first half of the inversion is already obvious to anyone building software today. The cost and speed of creating products has changed by orders of magnitude.

Companies that needed 100 engineers a year ago are shipping with a handful of people and AI agents. Cursor hit $1 billion ARR in 24 months — the fastest B2B SaaS ramp in history. Claude Code now authors 4% of all GitHub commits, projected to reach 20% by year-end. Over 100,000 products are built daily on AI-native platforms.

But Eric Schmidt and Innovation Endeavors co-founder Dror Berman, writing in [_When Machines Build for Machines_](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/when-machines-build-dror-berman-gtjoc), point to a subtler shift underneath the speed gains. When humans build, products are static — shipped in periodic releases because the cost of coordinating, gathering feedback, iterating, and deploying is high. When agents build, software can be generated on the fly, adapted per user, per context, in real time.

Push this further and the concept of a "product" itself starts to dissolve. If software can be generated for a specific user at a specific moment, there may be no product at all in the traditional sense — just a continuous act of creation that materializes when needed and reshapes itself each time.

The volume of code being written is about to increase by orders of magnitude. A growing share of it will never be seen or reviewed by a human. And that's where this gets really interesting — because every layer of our tech stack, from version control to programming languages, was designed assuming a human author. Those assumptions are breaking down.

### 🎯 The Consumption Side: Built FOR Agents

The second half of the inversion is less discussed but potentially more profound. When the end consumer of technology is no longer a person, the output no longer needs to be constrained by what a human can sense, comprehend, or attend to.

Schmidt and Berman use a medical example that makes this visceral. Since 1981, we've measured whether a cancer drug is working by having a radiologist measure tumor diameter — a single number extracted from an immensely complex 3D scan. Why? Because that's what humans can consistently interpret and communicate. An AI reading that scan doesn't need the tumor reduced to a diameter. It can work with the full dimensionality of the image, detecting patterns that predict treatment response far earlier and more accurately than the simplified measurement ever could.

The diameter was never the point. It was a compression artifact — a way to fit the complexity of a human body into the limits of a human mind.

That same compression is everywhere in technology. Source code is written for two audiences: the machine that executes it and the human who needs to understand it next. That second audience shaped everything — programming languages, naming conventions, architectural patterns designed for "maintainability." Schmidt and Berman land on a line that'll stick with you: **"Technical debt is really human-comprehension debt. Remove the human reader, and that debt disappears."**

### 📐 The Architectural Inversion

Here's where all the threads converge into a single insight.

For decades, software quality was measured by how well a platform _hid_ complexity from humans. Clean UI. Intuitive workflows. Abstract away the messy internals.

In the agentic era, software quality will be measured by how well a platform _exposes_ capability to machines.

This isn't theoretical. It's playing out in real time through the explosion of MCP (Model Context Protocol). Anthropic's open standard for connecting AI agents to tools has crossed 97 million monthly SDK downloads and been adopted by every major AI provider — OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon. Google just launched a Colab MCP Server. Microsoft shipped MCP support across Azure. The infrastructure for agents to consume services directly — without a human navigating a UI — is being built right now, at massive scale.

The question for every software company is no longer "how intuitive is our interface?" It's "how much capability can our agents access?" As one analysis put it: the competitive question changes from "which platform has the best interface?" to "which platform exposes the richest capabilities to agents?"

### 💰 The Business Model Collapse

If agents are the users, the entire pricing model of SaaS breaks.

Jensen Huang's GTC framing makes this explicit. SaaS — software as a service — was built for humans to log in, navigate, and act. GaaS — what Huang is calling the next era — is built for agents to receive instructions, plan execution, and deliver outcomes. The difference isn't cosmetic. It's structural.

Seat-based pricing becomes absurd when the "user" is an autonomous agent that can spin up a thousand instances. Salesforce and Intercom have already started moving toward outcome-based pricing — charging for tasks completed, tickets resolved, workflows closed rather than for access credentials.

Bain has mapped this transition across a three-layer stack: systems of record at the base, agent operating systems in the middle, and outcome interfaces at the top. The middle layer — where agents coordinate, plan, and execute — is where the value is migrating.

And the numbers don't lie about the disruption. $2 trillion in SaaS market cap evaporated in January 2026 alone. Software ETFs are down 30% since the start of the year. Jon Radoff's [_State of AI Agents in 2026_](https://meditations.metavert.io/p/the-state-of-ai-agents-in-2026) research found that while only 6% of organizations report meaningful EBIT impact from AI, there's a 6x output gap between top-quartile AI users and everyone else. The gap isn't closing — it's widening.

a16z's counter-argument is worth considering: code was never where the value lived. Network effects, brand, scale — the real moats — don't disappear just because agents are the users. Harvey and Hebbia are building collaboration spaces that connect humans _and their agents_ on single platforms. The more agents that use these platforms, the more valuable they become. Network effects might actually get _stronger_ in an agent-first world.

But the underlying shift is real. Software built for human comprehension is being replaced by software built for machine capability. The companies that recognize this early — and rebuild accordingly — will define the next era.

### 🔮 The Part That's Hard to Think About

Schmidt and Berman end their piece in genuinely disorienting territory. Push the logic of the inversion forward and you arrive somewhere most people aren't ready to reckon with.

We are building systems whose outputs may exceed not just our ability to produce them, but our ability to _evaluate_ them. A medical AI may reach conclusions no doctor can verify through traditional means. An agent may produce a codebase that works flawlessly but that no engineer fully understands. Claude's autonomous task horizon has gone from 4 minutes in early 2024 to 14.5 hours today — doubling every 123 days. At that rate, week-long autonomous tasks arrive by late 2026. Month-long tasks by mid-2027.

If expertise can be acquired on demand by an agent, what does a career built on knowledge accumulation actually mean? If an increasing share of valuable work is done by machines, what happens to economic systems built on pricing human time?

The constraint is no longer what we can build. As Jon Radoff put it: **"The bottleneck isn't engineering capacity anymore. It's imagination."**

When execution becomes abundant, the scarce resource shifts to the human who knows what to build, which problems matter, and what good looks like. The founders and teams that win will be the ones who see most clearly.

The floor is open.
