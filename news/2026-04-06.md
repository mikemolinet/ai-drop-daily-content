# Anthropic Hits $19B ARR as "Vibe Agents" Go Physical

Monday's bringing the heat with Anthropic's absolutely bonkers growth numbers and robots that can actually vibe their way through real-world tasks.

### 🔥 The Big Stories

**Anthropic's Revenue Growth Defies Physics**

The numbers are almost too crazy to believe: Anthropic went from $1B to $19B ARR in just one year, adding $6B in February alone. For context, Palantir and Atlassian took 15-20 years to reach $5B ARR — Anthropic is adding that every month. [Lenny's interview](https://x.com/lennysan/status/2040827067126907113) with their Head of Growth reveals they're talking about "exponential unlocking" as model capabilities compound with better tooling. The implication? We're still in the early innings of enterprise AI adoption, and the winners are winning really, really big.

**Physical "Vibe Agents" Enter the Chat**

While everyone's been obsessing over text-based AI agents, [Dr. Jim Fan dropped CaP-X](https://x.com/DrJimFan/status/2039358115318243352) — an open-source framework for "vibe agents" that control robot arms and humanoids. These aren't your typical rigid programmed robots; they use perception APIs, actuation APIs, and auto-synthesize skill libraries to navigate the physical world. The timing feels significant given all the chatter about AI agents hitting limitations — maybe the answer isn't better reasoning, but better embodiment.

**Small Models Hit a Wall on Agentic Tasks**

Ethan Mollick's [reality check on Gemma 4](https://x.com/emollick/status/2040925197767762425) cuts through the hype: while Google's latest on-device model is impressive for speed and basic tasks, it can't handle real agentic workflows. The core issue? "Model judgement, self-correction, and accuracy" — exactly what you need for agents that can operate independently. This suggests the agent revolution might remain cloud-dependent longer than the edge-computing crowd hoped.

### 📡 Quick Hits

**Naval Declares "Vibe Coding" More Addictive Than Gaming** — [The OG startup philosopher](https://x.com/naval/status/2039617101221224858) says building with AI assistance hits different than any video game ever made. The key caveat: "if you know what you want to build." Knowing what to build remains the human superpower.

**OpenAI Acquires a Podcast** — Sam Altman [confirmed the acquisition](https://x.com/sama/status/2039773754801422750) of "The Best Podcast Name" for what sources suggest is north of $100M. Because apparently buying talent and audience is easier than growing organically when you're sitting on infinite money.

**Three-Person Team Built $350M Note-Taking Empire** — Greg Isenberg [highlights Obsidian's success](https://x.com/gregisenberg/status/2040746181559501302) as proof that remote, tiny teams can build massive companies. The tools have never been better for small teams to punch above their weight.

**Token Limits = Performance Limits** — Mollick's follow-up shows [throwing more tokens at reasoning models](https://x.com/emollick/status/2040911007392903231) often yields better results. Raising Codex's token limit from 3M to 10M tripled independent work time on cybersecurity tasks. The lesson: benchmark performance might be more about resource constraints than model capabilities.

### 💡 Builder's Corner

**ThreeJS Becoming the Default Game Engine** — Andrew Chen [notes](https://x.com/andrewchen/status/2040955601887588655) that AI coding agents default to ThreeJS for game development, making it the inadvertent standard. If you're building anything game-like, consider what the AI tools will want to use.

**Better Corpus > Better Model** — Aadit Shah's [insight on Karpathy's knowledge base](https://x.com/aaditsh/status/2041105759350755647) success: it wasn't about the LLM, but about having 400K words of structured content in one directory. Most Fortune 500s already have this much internal knowledge — they just need to organize it properly.
