# Multi-AI Workflows Hit Mainstream as Agent Teams Unite

Monday's shaping up to be all about AI agents learning to work together — and the infrastructure they need to actually collaborate.

### 🔥 The Big Stories

**Agent Relay Launches as "Slack for AI Teams"**

Matt Shumer's new [Agent Relay](https://x.com/mattshumer_/status/2027605370470867280) tackles what might be the most overlooked piece of the AI agent puzzle: how do multiple agents actually coordinate? The platform provides channels, threads, DMs, and persistent history specifically for AI-to-AI communication. Shumer's betting this "will feel obvious in 12 months" — and he's probably right. As agents move beyond single-task execution to complex multi-agent workflows, they need structured ways to share context, delegate tasks, and maintain state across conversations. The timing aligns perfectly with enterprises realizing that one mega-agent isn't the answer; teams of specialized agents are.

**Karpathy's Multi-Agent Training Experiments Hit Reality**

Andrej Karpathy shared results from his [nanochat experiments](https://x.com/karpathy/status/2027521323275325622) running 8 agents (4 Claude, 4 Codex) on separate GPUs, each trying to remove logit softcap without regression. His blunt assessment: "it doesn't work and it's a mess." This is valuable real-world data on multi-agent training coordination. While the specific experiment failed, Karpathy's willingness to share negative results highlights how early we still are in figuring out agent-to-agent collaboration patterns. The infrastructure exists, but the orchestration strategies need serious work.

**Qwen 3.5 Claims Massive Model Compression Breakthrough**

The new [Qwen 3.5 lineup](https://x.com/itsPaulAi/status/2028463048915362140) is making bold claims: their 9B model matches GPT OSS 120B performance while being 13x smaller. If true, this represents a fundamental shift in the efficiency-vs-capability tradeoff. The 4B version allegedly matches the previous 80B model, with 0.8B and 2B variants designed for mobile. For builders, this could democratize powerful AI capabilities to run entirely offline on consumer hardware — no API costs, no latency, no privacy concerns.
